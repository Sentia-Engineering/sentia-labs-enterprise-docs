Population validation is the process of deciding whether a cohort behaves plausibly enough for the study you want to run. Some workspaces have access to a behavioral validation suite; every team should also do a practical research review before trusting a cohort.

## Practical validation checklist

Before using a population for an important simulation, review:

| Check | Healthy signal |
| --- | --- |
| **Audience fit** | The description, traits, and sample personas match the people you mean to study. |
| **Agent count** | The cohort has enough agents for the planned run. |
| **Research coverage** | Attached artifacts match the audience and topic. |
| **Demographic intent** | Location, age, education, occupation, and other filters are deliberate. |
| **Voice quality** | Sample personas and later simulation voices sound specific, not generic. |
| **Simulation history** | Prior runs did not reveal obvious audience mismatch. |

If any check fails, revise the population before interpreting results.

## Behavioral validation suite

Where enabled, Sentia can run agents through canonical behavioral economics experiments:

| Experiment | What it tests |
| --- | --- |
| **Dictator game** | Generosity and social norms. |
| **Trust game** | Trust and reciprocity. |
| **Public goods** | Cooperation under self-interest. |
| **Prisoner's dilemma** | Strategic cooperation. |
| **Ultimatum** | Fairness and rejection thresholds. |

These tests help detect cohorts that behave implausibly in broad human decision contexts. Passing them does not prove the cohort is right for your product; it only improves confidence that the agents are not obviously broken.

## Common failure patterns

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="The cohort sounds generic">
    The audience brief is too broad or lacks evidence. Narrow the brief and attach more relevant research artifacts.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="The cohort is too narrow">
    The population may not have enough useful variance. Duplicate it and broaden one dimension, such as role, geography, or experience level.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Agents miss known objections">
    The relevant research is absent or not attached. Add transcripts, survey responses, persona docs, or recordings that contain those objections.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="The same design gets contradictory results">
    Check whether the prompt changed, the cohort changed, or the design input changed. If the setup is stable, the study may be genuinely divided.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## What validation cannot tell you

- It cannot replace human research judgment.
- It cannot make weak or stale research artifacts reliable.
- It cannot validate a cohort outside its intended scope.
- It cannot prove a design will perform the same way after launch.

Treat validation as a readiness check, not a guarantee.

## When to revisit a population

Revisit or rebuild when:

- The audience definition changes.
- You upload better research.
- A report's voices feel off-audience.
- The cohort has been reused across too many different questions.
- Your team needs a sharper segment comparison.
