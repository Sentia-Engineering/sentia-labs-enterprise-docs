Before running real simulations against a population, validate that the agents behave like real humans. Sentia uses a standard suite of behavioral economics experiments — the same ones used in academic research on human decision-making.

## The 5-experiment suite

Each experiment puts agents in a well-studied decision context where human behavior follows known patterns. If your agents' behavior matches expected patterns, the population has high fidelity.

| Experiment | What it tests | Expected human pattern |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Dictator game** | Generosity & social norms | ~28% give away half; ~76% give something |
| **Trust game** | Trust & reciprocity | Trustees return ~50% of what's sent on average |
| **Public goods** | Cooperation under self-interest | Initial contribution ~40–60% of endowment, declining over rounds |
| **Prisoner's dilemma** | Strategic cooperation | ~50% cooperate in one-shot; cooperation increases when reputation is at stake |
| **Ultimatum** | Fairness & rejection | Proposals < 20% get rejected ~50% of the time |

If the population's responses are within tolerance of these patterns, fidelity is high. If they're not, the population is too generic, too narrow, or under-grounded.

## Running validation

Validation runs automatically on first build. You can re-run anytime.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open the population">
    Populations → click the population card. The first tab is **Validation**.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Click 'Run validation'">
    Sentia runs a 200-agent sample through all 5 experiments. ~2 minutes.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Read the report">
    The report shows pass rate, overall fidelity score, per-experiment results with reference distributions.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Reading the report

### Headline scores

| Metric | Healthy | What it means |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Pass rate** | ≥ 80% | Fraction of experiments where the population's behavior is within tolerance of the expected human pattern |
| **Fidelity score** | ≥ 0.7 | Composite score across all experiments, weighted by experiment importance |

Both need to be healthy for the population to be trusted in production simulations.

### Per-experiment view

Each experiment expands to show:

- **Distribution chart** — your agents' behavior overlaid on the expected human distribution
- **Statistical fit** — KL divergence between your agents' distribution and the reference
- **Pass/fail** — whether the divergence is within tolerance
- **Sample agent transcripts** — read 3–5 to spot-check reasoning

## Common failure patterns

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Pass rate 60–80%, fidelity 0.5–0.7 — under-grounded">
    The most common failure mode. Agents pass *some* experiments but show generic patterns on others.
    
    **Fix:** add or expand qualitative sources. Connect Dovetail or another support source. Rebuild.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Pass rate < 60% — bad scope">
    Population scope is wrong — too narrow (effective N too small), wrong region, or incompatible cohort filters.
    
    **Fix:** review filters. Broaden the cohort or split into multiple populations.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Pass rate 80%+, fidelity > 0.7, but agents 'sound off'">
    Quantitative validation passes but agent transcripts read poorly.
    
    **Fix:** check the qualitative corpus — are verbatims relevant to your domain? Filter to higher-relevance segments. Refresh embeddings.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="High variance across runs">
    Re-running validation produces different scores each time.
    
    **Fix:** the population's effective N is too small. Increase agent count, or reduce filter narrowness.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## What validation can't tell you

- It doesn't validate that agents will respond accurately to *your* specific product changes — that's what calibration is for
- It can't catch systematic biases inherited from biased qualitative data (e.g., if all your interviews are from happy customers)
- It can't validate a population for use outside its intended scope (an SMB population validated on SMB experiments won't transfer to enterprise scenarios)

Validation is necessary but not sufficient. **Calibration** is what closes the loop on real-world accuracy. See [Calibration](/calibration/overview).

## Re-validation cadence

Re-validate when:

- **Drift > 10%** (Sentia flags this automatically)
- **You refresh the qualitative corpus** (new interviews, ticket re-sync)
- **You edit filters** (validation reruns automatically)
- **Calibration accuracy drops** below your workspace's expected range

Re-validation is fast (~2 minutes) and free against contract allocation.

## DPD — bias detection

Beyond the behavioral suite, populations are also checked for **DPD (Demographic Parity Difference)** — a fairness metric that flags when protected demographic segments show statistically anomalous behavior.

If DPD exceeds the workspace threshold (default 0.10):

- An amber badge appears on the population
- Admins get a Slack notification
- The population is still usable, but high-stakes decisions should be reviewed manually

DPD is conservative. It can flag legitimate behavioral patterns. Your CSM can help interpret specific alerts.
