---

Population creation happens in the behavioral cohort studio. For first-week onboarding, the goal is not a giant model of every user. The goal is one focused cohort that can answer a real design question.

## Before you start

Prepare:

- The user group you want to simulate.
- The design or product questions this cohort should answer.
- Optional research artifacts in the [Resource Library](/resource-library/research-artifacts).
- Optional custom traits if your workspace needs them.

## Step 1 · Describe the audience

Write a concise natural-language brief:

```text
First-time enterprise workspace admins who are responsible for connecting Figma,
evaluating security implications, and proving value to a design leadership team.
```

The brief drives automatic suggestions for behavior patterns, psychological drivers, research contexts, and demographic filters.

## Step 2 · Choose cohort size

The UI supports **10-100 agents**.

| Size | Use when |
| --- | --- |
| **10-20** | Smoke tests and very narrow exploratory checks. |
| **30-50** | Recommended first-week range for design research. |
| **75-100** | Higher-variance audiences or reports that need more stable slices. |

Use more agents when you expect meaningful subgroup differences. Use fewer when you are testing setup quality or prompt clarity.

## Step 3 · Tune behavior and psychology

The composer can infer and let you adjust:

| Category | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| **Behavior patterns** | First-run confusion, habitual workarounds, setup abandonment, power-use shortcuts, price checking, comparison shopping, support escalation, collaboration handoffs. |
| **Psychological drivers** | Risk averse, novelty seeking, trust seeking, status conscious, control oriented, overwhelmed, skeptical of automation, privacy sensitive. |
| **Research contexts** | Onboarding, pricing, feature discovery, workflow change, trust moment, retention risk, upgrade path, accessibility review. |

Do not over-tag. Pick the patterns that explain the study, not every plausible user behavior.

## Step 4 · Set demographics

Available demographic grounding depends on geography:

| Geography | Available grounding |
| --- | --- |
| **United States** | ACS PUMS records, including age, gender, education, occupation, state, and race/ethnicity where configured. |
| **Global / non-US** | WorldPop and World Bank context with country, age, gender, education, and occupation fields. |

Demographics should support the research question. They should not become a substitute for behavior, task, or product context.

## Step 5 · Attach research artifacts

Attach transcripts, survey CSVs, persona docs, recordings, or experiment history from the Resource Library. These artifacts help agents sound and reason closer to the people your team studies.

Use artifacts when:

- The study is high-stakes.
- The cohort should reflect a specific audience's language.
- You have known pain points or objections from prior research.
- You want to avoid generic synthetic-user feedback.

## Step 6 · Build and review

Build the population, then open the detail page. Review:

- Sample personas.
- Behavior traits.
- Demographic panels.
- Qualitative coverage.
- Health signals.
- Any validation results available to the workspace.

If it does not read like the audience, revise the brief or attached artifacts before running important simulations.

## Updating an existing population

Common actions:

| Action | Use when |
| --- | --- |
| **Rename** | The use case becomes clearer after first runs. |
| **Duplicate** | You need a related cohort with one key difference. |
| **Archive** | The cohort should stay in history but not be reused. |
| **Delete** | The cohort is unused and wrong. Deletion can be blocked when simulations depend on it. |
