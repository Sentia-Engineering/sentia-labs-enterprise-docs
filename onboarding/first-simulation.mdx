title: "Run your first real simulation"
description: "Pick a real design question, use your first cohort, and read the report honestly."
---

This walkthrough assumes Figma is connected and you have one cohort. The goal is a real design research run — a question your team actually needs to answer.

The simulation wizard lives at `/simulations/new` and has **3 steps**: **Decision → Population → Configure**. Open it with the `N` shortcut, the **New simulation** button on the Simulations page, or — from the onboarding wizard — the **Create simulation** action.

<Note>
  Simulations are gated by the `simulations-page` feature flag. If `S` and `N` don't navigate anywhere and the sidebar is missing the **Simulations** item, ask your CSM to enable the flag.
</Note>

## Pick a question worth simulating

Good first candidates:

- An **onboarding redesign** — new first-run flow, removed steps, or setup sequence.
- A **trust moment** — permissions, security language, pricing, billing, or data access.
- A **navigation or IA change** — moving major sections, renaming, consolidation.
- A **variant comparison** — two or three designs with no team consensus.
- An **accessibility-sensitive flow** — places where comprehension or control matters.

Poor first candidates:

- Tiny copy tweaks disconnected from a real task.
- Bug fixes or parity work.
- Internal tooling your cohort would not use.
- Half-built concepts that do not represent a realistic user experience.

## Set up the simulation

<Steps>
  <Step title="Step 1 — Decision">
    Three sub-sections, in order:

    **Scenario type** (radio group, top of step):

    - **A/B Test** — compare options with confidence scores.
    - **Open-ended** — freeform feedback and concerns.
    - **Competitive** — compare against an alternative or incumbent.

    **Add context** (source picker — press `1` or `2` to select):

    - **Plain-text** (`1`) — paste the full context inline.
    - **Figma** (`2`) — paste a Figma file URL. If Figma is not connected, an inline alert links to **Settings → Integrations**.
    - **Coded Prototype** and **GitHub** are visible but disabled in the current rollout.

    For A/B scenarios, each variant gets its own context block (text or Figma URL).

    **What decision are you testing?** — the decision description textarea. Be concrete:

    - *"Which onboarding variant better helps first-time admins understand why Figma access is needed?"*
  </Step>

  <Step title="Step 2 — Population">
    Pick the population you built in [Build your first population](/onboarding/first-population). Confirm the audience matches the design question.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Step 3 — Configure">
    A three-column panel shows **Agents · Estimated cost · Estimated time** that updates as you change agent count.

    - **Agents** — minimum 10, maximum is the lower of 10,000 and your population's size.
    - **Estimated cost** — `$0.005/agent` in Fast mode (Haiku), `$0.012/agent` in Deep mode (Sonnet).
    - **Estimated time** — `max(2 min, agentCount / 60)`.

    Below that, the **Simulation Preview** shows top demographics, adopter mix, and 3 example personas drawn from the selected population.

    Press **Launch Simulation** (or `⌘⇧L`) to start. The launch overlay animates while the run is being created.
  </Step>
</Steps>

Use `⌘↵` to advance a step and `⌘⌫` to go back.

## Watch it run

The run page streams live results. Useful things to do:

- **Read live verbatims** to confirm agents understand the task.
- **Watch preference split** for A/B and competitive runs.
- **Watch concerns per agent** to see whether the design is creating friction.
- **Stop only if setup is wrong.** If the result is merely surprising, let it finish.

## Read results honestly

Completed runs open as a narrative report. For design research, read in this order:

<Steps>
  <Step title="1. Visual comparison">
    Confirm the correct Figma frames or design surfaces were evaluated. Do not analyze a report if the input is wrong.
  </Step>
  <Step title="2. Findings">
    Read the headline findings and what evidence supports them.
  </Step>
  <Step title="3. Concern hotspots">
    Look for confusion, trust concerns, and places where users hesitate. The accessibility audit sub-tab appears only when the `accessibility-audit` flag is enabled.
  </Step>
  <Step title="4. Population lens">
    Check whether reactions differ by trait, behavior pattern, demographic slice, or cohort subgroup.
  </Step>
  <Step title="5. Voices">
    Read representative quotes. If they sound generic or off-audience, improve the cohort before making a decision.
  </Step>
  <Step title="6. Recommendations">
    Treat recommendations as critique inputs, not automatic design instructions.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Share the result

**Export PDF** is gated by the `export-pdf-enabled` flag. With the flag on, the PDF includes the study question, hypothesis, configuration, evaluated surfaces, findings, concerns, recommendations, voices, population details, and the accessibility audit when available.

**A/B comparison** between two completed runs is gated by `compare-feature`. With the flag on, the **Compare** action appears on the Simulations list and on completed run pages.

Save important reports to the [Resource Library](/resource-library/reports) so the team can revisit the evidence.

## What to do with the answer

| Result | Action |
| --- | --- |
| Sentia confirms what you already believed | Use the report as one input in critique or planning. Keep the evidence, but do not skip human research judgment. |
| Sentia contradicts team consensus | Read concerns and voices carefully. Decide whether to revise, test with users, or run a sharper cohort. |
| Sentia is split or low confidence | Treat the design as contested. Look for segment patterns and consider a smaller human study or another variant. |

## Follow-up loop

After the first run:

- Revise the surface in Design Agent or Figma.
- Duplicate or rerun the simulation with a stronger prompt.
- Compare 2 completed runs (`compare-feature` flag).
- Save the comparison report if it changes the decision.
