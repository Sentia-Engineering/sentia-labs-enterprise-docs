---
title: "Build your first population"
description: "Create a focused behavioral cohort for the first design study."
---

A population is a reusable cohort of synthetic users. For the first study, keep it small and specific: one audience, one research purpose, and enough context that designers recognize the reactions.

The population composer is **prompt-first**: as you type the brief, Sentia infers behavior traits, psychology traits, research contexts, and demographic filters live. Manual edits to any of those fields "pin" them so further brief edits don't overwrite your choices.

## Before you start

Helpful inputs:

- A clear audience brief.
- Figma connected if the cohort will evaluate design work.
- Optional research artifacts to attach during creation (uploaded inline via the paper-clip in the composer, or staged in the [Resource Library](/resource-library/research-artifacts) when the `resource-library` flag is enabled).
- Optional design system guidance in **Settings → Design system**.

You can build the first cohort without uploaded research — add artifacts as soon as you have them; they make the cohort easier to trust.

## Pick your scope

Start narrow. The instinct to model "everyone" produces blurry cohorts that read flat. Good first-population scopes:

- **"First-time enterprise admins connecting Figma during onboarding"**
- **"UX researchers evaluating whether a prototype is ready for stakeholder review"**
- **"Procurement-sensitive buyers comparing pricing-page trust signals"**
- **"Accessibility-reliant users working through a setup flow"**

Each is specific enough for meaningful design feedback and broad enough to reuse across several simulations.

## Build it

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open the composer">
    Press `P` to go to Populations, then click **Create cohort** — or open `/populations/new` directly. From inside the onboarding wizard, **Open population builder** routes here.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Write the audience brief">
    Describe who the agents represent, what they are trying to do, and what context they bring. Example: *"First-time enterprise admins responsible for connecting Figma and proving value to design leadership."* The cohort name is derived from the brief; you can override it later.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Pick a size">
    Three presets — **Small (20)**, **Medium (50)**, **Large (100)** — or type any value between **10 and 100**. Default is **50**. Behavioral research needs a readable spread, not thousands of agents.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Tune the cohort (optional)">
    Sentia pre-fills behavior traits, psychology traits, research contexts, and demographic filters from the brief. Adjust any of them: toggle trait chips, set intensity, add your own, or refine the demographic filters. Manual edits pin that field — re-typing the brief won't overwrite it.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Attach research (optional)">
    Use the paper-clip control to upload transcripts, surveys, persona docs, recordings, or experiment exports. Uploaded artifacts are used to seed agent memories and language.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Create">
    Click the send button. Generation runs in the background. The onboarding wizard auto-advances to the next step once the population is marked done; from outside onboarding you'll see the population in `/populations`.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## What you'll see in the pre-launch preview

When you select this population in the simulation wizard's **Configure** step, Sentia renders a **Simulation Preview**:

- **Top demographics** (age, role, region, education, gender, income — whichever are most informative).
- **Adopter mix** (Innovator → Early Adopter → Early Majority → Late Majority → Laggard).
- **Country** (when set).
- **Example personas** — 3 representative agents with name, role, excerpt, and adopter category.

If the preview reads generic, narrow the brief and re-create. The brief is the strongest steering input.

## A common first-week pattern

Many new workspaces build two cohorts in week one:

1. A broad-enough "target users for onboarding" cohort (50 agents).
2. A narrower "enterprise admins with security concerns" or "new designers evaluating setup" cohort (30–50 agents).

You can keep multiple populations active. Simulations bind to one population each, so pick the cohort that best matches the design question.

## Maintenance

Refresh populations when:

- The target audience changes.
- You have better research artifacts to attach.
- A simulation report shows generic or implausible reasoning.
