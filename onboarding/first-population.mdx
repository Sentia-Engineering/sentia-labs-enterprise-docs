---
title: "Build your first population"
description: "Create a focused behavioral cohort for the first design study."
---

A population is a reusable cohort of synthetic users. For first-week onboarding, keep it small and specific: one audience, one research purpose, and enough context that designers recognize the reactions.

## Before you start

Helpful inputs:

- A clear audience brief.
- Figma connected if the cohort will evaluate design work.
- Optional research artifacts in the [Resource Library](/resource-library/research-artifacts).
- Optional design system guidance in [Settings -> Design system](/admin/design-system).

You can build the first cohort without uploaded research. Add artifacts as soon as you have them; they make the cohort easier to trust.

## Pick your scope

Start narrow. The instinct to model "everyone" produces blurry cohorts that read flat. Good first-population scopes:

- **"First-time enterprise admins connecting Figma during onboarding"**
- **"UX researchers evaluating whether a prototype is ready for stakeholder review"**
- **"Procurement-sensitive buyers comparing pricing-page trust signals"**
- **"Accessibility-reliant users working through a setup flow"**

Each scope is specific enough for meaningful design feedback and broad enough to reuse across several simulations.

## Build it

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open Populations → New">
    Press `P`, then create a new population.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Write the audience brief">
    Name the cohort and describe who the agents represent, what they are trying to do, and what context they bring. Example: "First-time enterprise admins responsible for connecting Figma and proving value to design leadership."
  </Step>

  <Step title="Choose size">
    Pick **10-100 agents**. Start with **50** unless you have a reason to go smaller or larger.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Tune the cohort">
    Add behavior patterns, psychological drivers, research contexts, and demographic filters. For US cohorts, state and race/ethnicity filters are available; global cohorts support age, gender, education, occupation, and country context.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Attach research">
    Add relevant transcripts, surveys, persona docs, recordings, or experiment history. Sentia uses these artifacts to seed the cohort's memories and language.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Build">
    Start the build. Generation runs in the background and the population becomes selectable for simulations when it reaches **Ready**.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Review the cohort

Open the population detail before using it for a high-stakes study. Look for:

- **Sample personas** that sound like the audience.
- **Behavior trait radar** that matches the study purpose.
- **Demographic mix** that is intentional, not accidental.
- **Qualitative coverage** showing which artifacts contributed.
- **Health signals** that indicate the population has usable agents.

<Tip>
  If the cohort reads generic, narrow the audience brief before adding more knobs. The brief is the strongest steering input.
</Tip>

## A common first-week pattern

Many new workspaces build two cohorts in week one:

1. A broad-enough "target users for onboarding" cohort (50 agents).
2. A narrower "enterprise admins with security concerns" or "new designers evaluating setup" cohort (30-50 agents).

You can keep multiple populations active. Simulations bind to one population each, so pick the cohort that best matches the design question.

## Maintenance

Maintain populations when:

- The target audience changes.
- You upload better research artifacts.
- The cohort has been reused for several decisions and the team has learned its blind spots.
- A simulation report shows generic or implausible reasoning.
